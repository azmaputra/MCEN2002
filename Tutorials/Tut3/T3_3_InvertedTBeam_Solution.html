Tutorial 3.3  –  Maximum Compressive Stress in an Inverted-T Beam

Problem Statement

Neglecting the weight of the beam, calculate the maximum compressive stress in the beam. The beam is simply supported (pin at the left end, roller 3 m away) and carries a 10 kN point load 1 m to the left of the roller. The cross-section is an inverted T: flange 11 cm × 1 cm at the bottom, stem 1 cm × 12 cm on top.

Given answer:   $\sigma_c = 156.83\ \text{MPa}$

Solution

1
Maximum bending moment
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Off-centre point load: $M_{\max} = Pab/L$ under the load

The load sits at $a = 2$ m from the pin and $b = 1$ m from the roller, on a span $L = 3$ m. Taking moments about A eliminates $R_A$ and gives $R_B$ directly:

$$\sum M_A = 0:\quad R_B\,(3) - 10\,(2) = 0$$ $$R_B = \frac{Pa}{L} = \frac{10(2)}{3} = 6.667\ \text{kN}$$

Then vertical equilibrium gives $R_A$:

$$\sum F_y = 0:\quad R_A + R_B - 10 = 0$$ $$R_A = 10 - 6.667 = 3.333\ \text{kN} \;\left(= \frac{Pb}{L}\right)$$

The bending moment rises linearly from each support and peaks under the load:

$$M_{\max} = \frac{Pab}{L} = \frac{10 \times 2 \times 1}{3} = 6.667\ \text{kN}\!\cdot\!\text{m} = 6.667 \times 10^6\ \text{N}\!\cdot\!\text{mm}$$

Sanity check: $M_{\max} = R_A \cdot a = 3.333 \times 2 = 6.667$ kN·m from the left, and $R_B \cdot b = 6.667 \times 1 = 6.667$ kN·m from the right — both paths agree. ✓ The moment is positive (sagging) everywhere between the supports.

2
Locate the centroid of the inverted-T section
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Two rectangles, working in mm from the bottom face

Convert to mm and split into flange ① ($110 \times 10$, $y = 0$–$10$) and stem ② ($10 \times 120$, $y = 10$–$130$):

$$A = 110(10) + 10(120) = 1\,100 + 1\,200 = 2\,300\ \text{mm}^2$$
$$\bar{y} = \frac{1\,100(5) + 1\,200(70)}{2\,300}$$ $$= \frac{5\,500 + 84\,000}{2\,300} = \frac{89\,500}{2\,300}$$

$$\bar{y} = 38.91\ \text{mm above the bottom} \;\Rightarrow\; y_t = 38.91\ \text{mm}, \quad y_c = 130 - 38.91 = 91.09\ \text{mm}$$

Physical check: the areas are nearly equal (1 100 vs 1 200 mm²), so the centroid falls roughly midway between their centroids (5 and 70 mm) — a little above 37.5 mm. ✓

3
Second moment of area about the centroidal x–x axis
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Parallel axis theorem on flange and stem

Distances from each part's centroid to $\bar{y} = 38.91$ mm: flange $d_1 = 38.91 - 5 = 33.91$ mm; stem $d_2 = 70 - 38.91 = 31.09$ mm.

$$\text{(1) flange: } \frac{110(10)^3}{12} + 1\,100(33.91)^2 = 9\,167 + 1\,264\,873 = 1.274 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$
$$\text{(2) stem: } \frac{10(120)^3}{12} + 1\,200(31.09)^2 = 1\,440\,000 + 1\,159\,910 = 2.600 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

$$I_{xx} = 1.274 \times 10^6 + 2.600 \times 10^6 = 3.874 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4 \;(= 387.4\ \text{cm}^4)$$

4
Maximum compressive stress
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Sagging → compression at the top; the stem tip is the extreme fibre

The sagging moment puts the top fibre (the stem tip, $y_c = 91.09$ mm) in compression and the bottom face ($y_t = 38.91$ mm) in tension:

The extreme compressive fibre is much farther from the neutral axis ($91.09$ vs $38.91$ mm), so compression is the larger stress:

$$\sigma_c = \frac{M_{\max}\, y_c}{I} = \frac{6.667 \times 10^6 \times 91.09}{3.874 \times 10^6}$$

$$\sigma_c = 156.8\ \text{MPa (compression, at the top of the stem, under the load)}$$

For comparison, the bottom-fibre tensile stress at the same section:

$$\sigma_t = \frac{6.667 \times 10^6 \times 38.91}{3.874 \times 10^6} = 66.97\ \text{MPa}$$

On the book value 156.83 MPa: the exact result is $\sigma_c = 156.75$ MPa (with $\bar{y} = 89\,500/2\,300$ mm kept exact). Rounding $\bar{y}$ and $I$ at intermediate steps produces the book's 156.83 — a 0.05% difference. Both are acceptable; carry exact fractions if you want to match 156.75.

Common mistake 1: Using $M = PL/4$ as if the load were at mid-span. The load is at the third point ($a = 2$ m, $b = 1$ m), so $M_{\max} = Pab/L = 6.667$ kN·m, not $PL/4 = 7.5$ kN·m. The mid-span formula overestimates the moment by 12.5% here.

Common mistake 2: Taking the extreme fibre at half the depth ($65$ mm). The inverted-T is strongly asymmetric — the neutral axis is only 38.91 mm above the bottom, so the fibre distances are 91.09 mm (top) and 38.91 mm (bottom), not 65 mm each.

Common mistake 3: Mixing cm and mm. The figure is dimensioned in cm; the cleanest route is to convert everything to mm at the start (N, mm, mm⁴ → MPa directly). Computing $I$ in cm⁴ and forgetting the factor $10^4$ when converting to mm⁴ changes the stress by four orders of magnitude — an error a quick "does 0.016 MPa make sense for a 10 kN load?" check would catch.
