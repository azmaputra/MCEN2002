Tutorial 3.2  –  Moment of Resistance of an Extruded Section

Problem Statement

The figure shows the cross-section of an extruded alloy member used as a simply supported beam with the 60 mm wide flange at the bottom. Determine the moment of resistance of the section if the maximum permissible stresses in tension and compression are 60 and 45 MPa respectively. The loading causes a positive (sagging) moment, e.g. a distributed load.

Given answer:   $M_{\max} = 1.322\ \text{kN}\!\cdot\!\text{m}$

Solution

1
Read the figure: decompose the section into four rectangles
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Fix the geometry before touching any formula

Measuring $y$ upward from the bottom face, the section is four rectangles:

$$\begin{aligned} &\text{(1) bottom flange: } 60 \times 5 \quad (y = 0 \text{ to } 5)\\ &\text{(2) web: } 5 \times 50 \quad (y = 5 \text{ to } 55)\\ &\text{(3) wide flange: } 140 \times 5 \quad (y = 55 \text{ to } 60)\\ &\text{(4) stub: } 20 \times 20 \quad (y = 60 \text{ to } 80) \end{aligned}$$

Reading the 60 mm height dimension: in the figure it runs from the bottom face up to the top of the 140 mm flange (its extension line meets the 5 mm thickness dimension). The web height is therefore $60 - 5 - 5 = 50$ mm and the overall depth is $60 + 20 = 80$ mm. This reading is confirmed by the book answer — it reproduces $M = 1.322$ kN·m exactly (see Step 4).

Total area:

$$A = 300 + 250 + 700 + 400 = 1\,650\ \text{mm}^2$$
2
Locate the centroid
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 First moment of each rectangle about the bottom face

Each rectangle's centroid height: ① $2.5$ mm, ② $5 + 25 = 30$ mm, ③ $55 + 2.5 = 57.5$ mm, ④ $60 + 10 = 70$ mm.

$$\bar{y} = \frac{300(2.5) + 250(30) + 700(57.5) + 400(70)}{1\,650}$$ $$= \frac{750 + 7\,500 + 40\,250 + 28\,000}{1\,650} = \frac{76\,500}{1\,650}$$

$$\bar{y} = 46.36\ \text{mm above the bottom} \;\Rightarrow\; y_t = 46.36\ \text{mm}, \quad y_c = 80 - 46.36 = 33.64\ \text{mm}$$

Physical check: most of the area (the 140 mm flange and the stub) is concentrated in the top quarter of the section, so the centroid must sit well above mid-depth (40 mm) — 46.36 mm is consistent. ✓

3
Second moment of area about the centroidal x–x axis
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Parallel axis theorem, part by part

For each rectangle, $I = \dfrac{bh^3}{12} + Ad^2$ with $d$ the distance from its own centroid to $\bar{y} = 46.36$ mm:

$$\text{(1): } \frac{60(5)^3}{12} + 300(43.86)^2 = 625 + 577\,206 = 0.5778 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$
$$\text{(2): } \frac{5(50)^3}{12} + 250(16.36)^2 = 52\,083 + 66\,942 = 0.1190 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$
$$\text{(3): } \frac{140(5)^3}{12} + 700(11.14)^2 = 1\,458 + 86\,813 = 0.0883 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$
$$\text{(4): } \frac{20(20)^3}{12} + 400(23.64)^2 = 13\,333 + 223\,471 = 0.2368 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

$$I_{xx} = (0.5778 + 0.1190 + 0.0883 + 0.2368) \times 10^6 = 1.022 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

Insight: the transfer terms $Ad^2$ dominate — the thin bottom flange alone contributes 57% of $I_{xx}$ almost entirely through its $Ad^2$ (its own $bh^3/12$ is a negligible 625 mm⁴). Thin-walled sections work by placing area far from the neutral axis.

4
Moment of resistance: two limits, the smaller governs
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Compute a candidate $M$ for each stress limit, then take the minimum

A positive (sagging) moment puts the bottom fibre in tension ($\sigma_t \le 60$ MPa at $y_t = 46.36$ mm) and the top fibre in compression ($\sigma_c \le 45$ MPa at $y_c = 33.64$ mm). Each limit gives a candidate moment $M = \sigma I / y$:

$$M_t = \frac{60 \times 1.022 \times 10^6}{46.36} = 1.3225 \times 10^6\ \text{N}\!\cdot\!\text{mm} = 1.322\ \text{kN}\!\cdot\!\text{m}$$
$$M_c = \frac{45 \times 1.022 \times 10^6}{33.64} = 1.3672 \times 10^6\ \text{N}\!\cdot\!\text{mm} = 1.367\ \text{kN}\!\cdot\!\text{m}$$

The section can only carry the moment that satisfies both limits simultaneously — the smaller candidate:

$$M_{\max} = \min(1.322,\ 1.367) = 1.322\ \text{kN}\!\cdot\!\text{m} \quad \text{(tension governs)}$$

5
Verify the non-governing fibre
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 5 Check the compression stress under $M = 1.322$ kN·m

Under the governing moment, the top-fibre compressive stress is:

$$\sigma_c = \frac{M\, y_c}{I} = \frac{1.3225 \times 10^6 \times 33.64}{1.022 \times 10^6} = 43.5\ \text{MPa} \;<\; 45\ \text{MPa} \ \checkmark$$

Both limits are satisfied; the bottom fibre sits exactly at 60 MPa while the top retains a small margin.

Design insight: the two candidates (1.322 vs 1.367) are within 3.3% of each other — this extrusion is close to a balanced design, where both fibres reach their limits at the same moment. That is exactly why the section is asymmetric: the ideal centroid position for simultaneous failure satisfies $y_t/y_c = \sigma_t/\sigma_c = 60/45 = 1.33$, and this section has $y_t/y_c = 46.36/33.64 = 1.38$. The extra area at the top (wide flange + stub) deliberately pulls the neutral axis toward the compression face.

Common mistake 1: Taking the larger candidate moment (1.367 kN·m). At that moment the bottom fibre would carry $60 \times 1.367/1.322 = 62$ MPa — above its tensile limit. The moment of resistance is always the minimum over all stress limits.

Common mistake 2: Pairing the limits with the wrong fibres. The problem states the 60 mm flange is at the bottom and the moment is positive (sagging), so tension (60 MPa) belongs to the bottom fibre at 46.36 mm and compression (45 MPa) to the top at 33.64 mm. Swapping them gives $\min(60 \cdot I/33.64,\ 45 \cdot I/46.36) = 0.992$ kN·m — wrong value and wrong governing fibre.

Common mistake 3: Misreading the 60 mm vertical dimension as the overall depth. That makes the web $30$ mm tall, the depth 60 mm, and gives $M \approx 0.75$ kN·m — nowhere near the book answer. When a figure is ambiguous, check your reading against the given answer before committing.
