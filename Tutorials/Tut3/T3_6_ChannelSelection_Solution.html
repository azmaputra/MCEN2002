Tutorial 3.6  –  Selecting a Parallel Flange Channel; I about the Bottom Axis

Problem Statement

Using the manufacturer's table for Parallel Flange Channels (PFC):

a) Select a channel able to withstand a maximum bending moment of 50 kN·m about its neutral axis with a safety factor of 1.5 against a maximum yield stress of 300 MPa.
b) For the channel found in (a), use the parallel axis theorem to calculate the second moment of area about the bottom axis $k$.

Given answer:   a) 250 PFC    b) $I_k = 115.725 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$

Solution

1
Part (a): required section modulus
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Apply the safety factor to the yield stress, then invert $\sigma = M/Z$

The safety factor caps the working stress at:

$$\sigma_{\text{allow}} = \frac{\sigma_y}{SF} = \frac{300}{1.5} = 200\ \text{MPa}$$

The bending stress at the extreme fibre is $\sigma = M/Z_x$, so the channel must provide at least:

$$Z_{\text{req}} = \frac{M}{\sigma_{\text{allow}}} = \frac{50 \times 10^6\ \text{N}\!\cdot\!\text{mm}}{200\ \text{N/mm}^2}$$

$$Z_{\text{req}} = 250 \times 10^3\ \text{mm}^3$$

Unit check: 50 kN·m $= 50 \times 10^6$ N·mm; dividing N·mm by N/mm² gives mm³. ✓

2
Part (a): select the lightest adequate channel from the table
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Scan the $Z_x$ column for the smallest value ≥ 250 × 10³ mm³

Compare $Z_{\text{req}} = 250 \times 10^3$ mm³ against the elastic modulus $Z_x$ column (channels are listed heaviest-first, so scan from the bottom up for the most economical pick):

Designation Mass (kg/m) $Z_x$ (10³ mm³) $Z_x \ge 250 \times 10^3$?
230 PFC 25.1 233 ✗ fails (233 < 250)
250 PFC 35.5 361 ✓ smallest that passes
300 PFC 40.1 483 ✓ but heavier than needed

$$\textbf{Select 250 PFC} \quad (Z_x = 361 \times 10^3\ \text{mm}^3,\ 35.5\ \text{kg/m})$$

Actual margin: under 50 kN·m the working stress is $\sigma = 50 \times 10^6 / 361 \times 10^3 = 138.5$ MPa, an actual safety factor of $300/138.5 = 2.17$. Discrete catalogue sizes mean you rarely land exactly on the required 1.5 — the requirement is a minimum.

3
Part (b): parallel axis theorem to the bottom axis k
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 $I_k = I_x + A\,\bar{y}^2$ with $\bar{y} = d/2$

The 250 PFC is symmetric about x–x, so its centroid is at mid-depth: $\bar{y} = 250/2 = 125$ mm above the bottom axis $k$. From the table, $I_x = 45.1 \times 10^6$ mm⁴ and $A_g = 4\,520$ mm². The parallel axis theorem transfers from the centroidal axis outward:

$$I_k = I_x + A\,\bar{y}^2$$ $$= 45.1 \times 10^6 + 4\,520\,(125)^2 = 45.1 \times 10^6 + 70.625 \times 10^6$$

$$I_k = 115.725 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

Physical check: the transfer term ($70.6 \times 10^6$) exceeds the centroidal value ($45.1 \times 10^6$) — moving the reference axis 125 mm away from the centroid more than doubles $I$. $I$ is always a minimum about the centroidal axis, so $I_k > I_x$ necessarily. ✓

4
Summary Table
Step 4 Both parts at a glance
Quantity Expression Value
Allowable stress $\sigma_y / SF = 300/1.5$ 200 MPa
Required modulus $Z_{\text{req}} = M/\sigma_{\text{allow}}$ 250 × 10³ mm³
Selected channel smallest $Z_x \ge Z_{\text{req}}$ 250 PFC ($Z_x$ = 361 × 10³ mm³)
Transfer distance $\bar{y} = d/2$ 125 mm
Second moment about k $I_k = I_x + A\bar{y}^2$ 115.725 × 10⁶ mm⁴

Common mistake 1: Skipping the safety factor and requiring only $Z \ge 50 \times 10^6/300 = 166.7 \times 10^3$ mm³. The 230 PFC ($233 \times 10^3$) would then appear adequate — but at 50 kN·m it would run at 215 MPa, a safety factor of only 1.4 against yield.

Common mistake 2: Reading the plastic modulus $S_x$ instead of the elastic $Z_x$. The 230 PFC's $S_x = 271 \times 10^3 \ge 250 \times 10^3$ would wrongly pass the check; elastic design with $\sigma = M/Z$ must use the $Z_x$ column.

Common mistake 3: Using the full depth ($d = 250$ mm) instead of $d/2 = 125$ mm as the transfer distance in part (b). The distance in $A\bar{y}^2$ runs from the centroid to the new axis — that error would give $I_k = 327.6 \times 10^6$ mm⁴, nearly triple the correct value. Remember also that the theorem only transfers from the centroidal axis; never chain it between two non-centroidal axes.
