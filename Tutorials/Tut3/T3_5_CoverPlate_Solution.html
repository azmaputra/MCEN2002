Tutorial 3.5  –  Cover Plate on a Universal Beam: Section Modulus Increase

Problem Statement

A steel cover plate of cross-section 100 mm × 12 mm is welded along the full length of the top flange of a 360 UB 56.7 universal beam. What is the percentage increase in section modulus $Z_x$ compared to the universal beam alone?

Given answer:   $6.13\%$

From Table 13 (Universal Beams), 360 UB 56.7: depth $d = 359$ mm, area $A = 7\,240$ mm², $I_x = 161 \times 10^6$ mm⁴, $Z_x = 899 \times 10^3$ mm³ (flange 172 × 13.0 mm, web 8.0 mm).

Solution

1
Baseline: the universal beam alone
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Read $I_x$ and $Z_x$ straight from the section table

The UB is doubly symmetric, so its neutral axis is at mid-depth and both fibres sit at $d/2 = 179.5$ mm. From Table 13:

$$A_{UB} = 7\,240\ \text{mm}^2 \qquad I_{UB} = 161 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4 \qquad Z_{UB} = 899 \times 10^3\ \text{mm}^3$$

Consistency check: $Z = I/(d/2) = 161 \times 10^6 / 179.5 = 897 \times 10^3$ mm³ ≈ the tabulated $899 \times 10^3$ (the small difference is table rounding of $I_x$). ✓ Note the table's $S_x = 1\,010 \times 10^3$ mm³ is the plastic modulus — not what we want here.

2
Centroid of the composite section
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Treat the UB as one lump at mid-depth, the plate as another

Measure $y$ from the bottom of the UB. The UB's centroid is at $179.5$ mm; the plate ($A_p = 100 \times 12 = 1\,200$ mm²) sits on top of the beam, centroid at $359 + 6 = 365$ mm:

$$\bar{y} = \frac{A_{UB}\,(179.5) + A_p\,(365)}{A_{UB} + A_p}$$ $$= \frac{7\,240(179.5) + 1\,200(365)}{8\,440} = \frac{1\,299\,580 + 438\,000}{8\,440}$$

$$\bar{y} = 205.87\ \text{mm} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{the NA rises } 26.37\ \text{mm above mid-depth}$$

Fibre distances of the composite (overall depth $359 + 12 = 371$ mm):

$$y_{bot} = 205.87\ \text{mm} \qquad y_{top} = 371 - 205.87 = 165.13\ \text{mm}$$
3
Second moment of area of the composite
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Parallel axis theorem on the UB and on the plate

Both parts transfer to the new NA at $\bar{y} = 205.87$ mm. The UB shifts by $26.37$ mm, the plate by $365 - 205.87 = 159.13$ mm:

$$I_{UB \to NA} = 161 \times 10^6 + 7\,240(26.37)^2 = 161 \times 10^6 + 5.04 \times 10^6 = 166.04 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$
$$I_{p \to NA} = \frac{100(12)^3}{12} + 1\,200(159.13)^2 = 0.014 \times 10^6 + 30.39 \times 10^6 = 30.40 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

$$I_{xx} = 166.04 \times 10^6 + 30.40 \times 10^6 = 196.4 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

Insight: the plate's own $bh^3/12$ (14 400 mm⁴) is utterly negligible — 0.05% of its transfer term. A thin cover plate works almost entirely through $Ad^2$, exactly like the flanges of the UB itself.

4
Section modulus of the composite and the percentage increase
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 $Z$ uses the fibre farthest from the NA — now the bottom

The section modulus is $Z = I/y_{\max}$ with $y_{\max}$ the larger fibre distance. Because the NA moved up, the bottom fibre is now the critical one:

$$Z_{bot} = \frac{196.4 \times 10^6}{205.87} = 954.2 \times 10^3\ \text{mm}^3 \qquad \left(Z_{top} = \frac{196.4 \times 10^6}{165.13} = 1\,189.6 \times 10^3\ \text{mm}^3\right)$$
$$Z_x = \min(Z_{bot},\ Z_{top}) = 954.2 \times 10^3\ \text{mm}^3$$

Percentage increase over the bare UB:

$$\frac{Z_x - Z_{UB}}{Z_{UB}} \times 100 = \frac{954.2 - 899}{899} \times 100$$

$$\text{Increase in } Z_x = 6.13\%$$

Design insight: the plate adds 16.6% more area but buys only 6.13% more elastic strength — poor value compared to plating both flanges (which would keep the NA central and raise $Z$ by ~19%). A single cover plate mainly stiffens the section ($I$ up 22%) while the shifted NA eats most of the strength gain at the bottom fibre. This is why single cover plates are used to fix a local stress problem at one flange, not to upgrade a beam overall.

Common mistake 1: Computing $Z$ at the top fibre because "that's where the plate is." $Z_x$ is defined by the fibre farthest from the NA — the bottom one here ($205.9 > 165.1$ mm). Using $Z_{top} = 1\,190 \times 10^3$ mm³ would claim a 32% increase, five times the real benefit.

Common mistake 2: Forgetting the NA shift and evaluating $I/(d/2 + t_p)$ or adding the plate's $Ad^2$ about the old mid-depth axis. The composite NA must be found first (Step 2); every transfer distance is measured from it.

Common mistake 3: Reading $S_x = 1\,010 \times 10^3$ mm³ from the table instead of $Z_x = 899 \times 10^3$ mm³. In Australian section tables $Z$ is the elastic modulus and $S$ the plastic modulus — the naming is reversed in US/UK texts, so check the column heading, not the symbol you're used to.
