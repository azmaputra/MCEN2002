Tutorial 3.1  –  Cast Iron Beam: Maximum Allowable Span

Problem Statement

A cast iron beam of the trapezoidal section shown is simply supported at its ends and carries a load of 18 kN at mid-span. Find the maximum allowable span if the stress due to bending is not to exceed 30 MPa in tension. Neglect the weight of the beam. What will be the maximum compressive stress?

Given answer:   $L = 1.056\ \text{m}$,   $\sigma_c = 42\ \text{MPa}$

The book prints this as "$I = 1.056$ m" — that value is the span $L$ (the symbol/unit is a typo; $I$ is in mm⁴).

Solution

1
Locate the centroid of the trapezoidal section
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Split into a rectangle + two triangles, measure y from the bottom face

Split the trapezoid into a central rectangle ($30 \times 120$) and two side triangles (each base 30 mm, height 120 mm, wide end at the bottom). Measuring $y$ upward from the bottom face:

$$A_{\text{rect}} = 30 \times 120 = 3\,600\ \text{mm}^2, \quad y_{\text{rect}} = 60\ \text{mm}$$
$$A_{\text{tri}} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 30 \times 120 = 1\,800\ \text{mm}^2 \ \text{(each)}, \quad y_{\text{tri}} = \tfrac{120}{3} = 40\ \text{mm}$$

Total area and centroid:

$$A = 3\,600 + 2(1\,800) = 7\,200\ \text{mm}^2$$
$$\bar{y} = \frac{3\,600(60) + 2 \times 1\,800(40)}{7\,200} = \frac{216\,000 + 144\,000}{7\,200} = 50\ \text{mm}$$

$$\bar{y} = 50\ \text{mm above the bottom face} \quad\Rightarrow\quad y_t = 50\ \text{mm}, \quad y_c = 120 - 50 = 70\ \text{mm}$$

Physical check: the section is wider at the bottom, so the centroid must sit below mid-height (60 mm) — 50 mm is consistent. ✓  The direct trapezoid formula agrees: $\bar{y} = \dfrac{h}{3}\dfrac{2a+b}{a+b} = \dfrac{120}{3}\dfrac{2(30)+90}{30+90} = 50$ mm, with $a = 30$ (top), $b = 90$ (bottom).

2
Second moment of area about the centroidal x–x axis
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Parallel axis theorem on each part

Each part's own centroidal value plus its transfer term $Ad^2$, where $d$ is the distance from the part's centroid to the section centroid at $\bar{y} = 50$ mm:

Rectangle ($d = 60 - 50 = 10$ mm):

$$I_{\text{rect}} = \frac{30 \times 120^3}{12} + 3\,600(10)^2 = 4.320 \times 10^6 + 0.360 \times 10^6 = 4.680 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

Each triangle ($d = 50 - 40 = 10$ mm):

$$I_{\text{tri}} = \frac{30 \times 120^3}{36} + 1\,800(10)^2 = 1.440 \times 10^6 + 0.180 \times 10^6 = 1.620 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

Total:

$$I_{xx} = 4.680 \times 10^6 + 2(1.620 \times 10^6)$$

$$I_{xx} = 7.92 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$$

Cross-check: the direct trapezoid formula $I = \dfrac{h^3(a^2 + 4ab + b^2)}{36(a+b)} = \dfrac{120^3(900 + 10\,800 + 8\,100)}{36(120)} = 7.92 \times 10^6$ mm⁴ agrees exactly. ✓

3
Which fibre is in tension? Why tension governs cast iron
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Sagging beam → bottom in tension; the 30 MPa limit applies there

A simply supported beam with a downward mid-span load sags: the bottom fibre stretches (tension), the top fibre shortens (compression). The extreme fibre distances are:

$$y_t = 50\ \text{mm (bottom, tension)} \qquad y_c = 70\ \text{mm (top, compression)}$$

Why the problem limits tension, not compression: cast iron is a brittle material, several times stronger in compression than in tension. Failure therefore initiates on the tension side, so the design condition is $\sigma_t \le 30$ MPa at the bottom fibre — even though the top fibre, being farther from the neutral axis ($70 > 50$ mm), carries the numerically larger stress. This is also why the section is oriented wide-face-down: it keeps the neutral axis close to the tension face.

4
Maximum allowable span
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Set $\sigma_t = 30$ MPa at the bottom fibre, with $M = PL/4$

For a central point load on a simple span, the maximum bending moment is at mid-span:

$$M_{\max} = \frac{PL}{4} = \frac{18\,000\,L}{4} = 4\,500\,L \quad (\text{N}\!\cdot\!\text{mm, with } L \text{ in mm})$$

Apply the bending stress formula at the tension (bottom) fibre and set it to the limit:

$$\sigma_t = \frac{M_{\max}\, y_t}{I} \quad\Rightarrow\quad 30 = \frac{4\,500\,L \times 50}{7.92 \times 10^6}$$
$$L = \frac{30 \times 7.92 \times 10^6}{4\,500 \times 50} = 1\,056\ \text{mm}$$

$$L_{\max} = 1\,056\ \text{mm} = 1.056\ \text{m}$$

Unit check: with $P$ in N, $L$ in mm, $y$ in mm and $I$ in mm⁴, the stress comes out in N/mm² = MPa directly — no conversion factors needed. The corresponding moment is $M_{\max} = 4\,500 \times 1\,056 = 4.752 \times 10^6$ N·mm $= 4.752$ kN·m.

5
Maximum compressive stress
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 5 Same $M$, top fibre at $y_c = 70$ mm

At the same section and moment, the top fibre is farther from the neutral axis, so it carries a larger stress — but in compression:

$$\sigma_c = \frac{M_{\max}\, y_c}{I} = \frac{4.752 \times 10^6 \times 70}{7.92 \times 10^6}$$

$$\sigma_c = 42\ \text{MPa}$$

Shortcut: stress scales linearly with distance from the neutral axis, so $\sigma_c = \sigma_t \times \dfrac{y_c}{y_t} = 30 \times \dfrac{70}{50} = 42$ MPa — no need to recompute $M/I$. For cast iron (compressive strength typically 3–4× the tensile strength), 42 MPa in compression is comfortably safe while the tension side sits exactly at its limit.

Common mistake 1: Using half the depth ($y = 60$ mm) for both fibres, as if the section were symmetric. The trapezoid's neutral axis is at 50 mm from the bottom, not mid-height. Using 60 mm for the tension fibre gives $L = 880$ mm — 17% too short — and hides the fact that the two fibre stresses differ.

Common mistake 2: Applying the 30 MPa limit to the fibre farthest from the neutral axis (the top, 70 mm) out of habit. That fibre is in compression; the stated limit is a tensile limit and belongs to the bottom fibre at 50 mm. Applying it at 70 mm gives $L = 754$ mm and wastes the beam's capacity.

Common mistake 3: Using $M = PL$ or $M = PL/2$ instead of $M = PL/4$ for a central point load on a simple span. Each support reaction is $P/2$, and the moment at mid-span is $(P/2)(L/2) = PL/4$. Using $PL/2$ halves the allowable span to 528 mm.
