Tutorial 2.1  –  Shear Force and Bending Moment Diagrams

Problem Statement

Draw the shear force and bending moment diagrams for the loaded beam shown below. What are the values of the shear force and bending moment at the middle of the beam?

Given answer:   $V = -400\ \text{N}$     $M = 3400\ \text{N}\cdot\text{m}$

Strategy: Find the support reactions using overall equilibrium ($\sum F_y = 0$, $\sum M = 0$), then build $V(x)$ and $M(x)$ segment by segment, remembering that a point force causes a jump in the shear diagram and a concentrated applied moment causes a jump in the bending moment diagram (but not in the shear diagram).

Solution

1
Find the support reactions
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Apply $\sum M_A = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$

Take moments about A (counter-clockwise positive). The 4 kN load acts 1 m from A, the roller reaction $R_B$ acts 3 m from A, and the applied 2800 N·m moment (shown counter-clockwise in the figure) acts anywhere along the beam — its effect is the same regardless of location:

$$\sum M_A = 0: \quad R_B(3) - 4(1) + 2.8 = 0$$ $$R_B = \frac{4(1) - 2.8}{3} = \frac{1.2}{3} = 0.4\text{ kN} = 400\text{ N}$$

Then, summing vertical forces:

$$\sum F_y = 0: \quad R_A + R_B = 4\text{ kN}$$ $$R_A = 4 - 0.4 = 3.6\text{ kN} = 3600\text{ N}$$

$R_A = 3.6\text{ kN (up)}$     $R_B = 0.4\text{ kN (up)}$

Check: Both reactions come out positive, so both act upward as assumed — the load and applied moment are held in equilibrium by a much larger reaction at A than at B, since A is close to the 4 kN load.

2
Construct the shear force diagram $V(x)$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Track $V(x)$ segment by segment — jumps occur only at point forces

Starting from A and moving right, $V(x)$ equals the sum of vertical forces encountered so far (reactions up, loads down). The concentrated moment does not change $V$.

① From A to the load ($0 \le x < 1$):

$$V(x) = R_A = 3.6\text{ kN}$$

② From the load to B ($1 < x \le 3$):

$$V(x) = R_A - 4 = 3.6 - 4 = -0.4\text{ kN} = -400\text{ N}$$

This value stays constant across the applied-moment location at $x = 2$ m — moments create no shear jump. At $x = 3$ m, $R_B = 0.4$ kN restores $V$ to zero, closing the diagram.

Shear force at mid-span ($x = 1.5$ m): $V = -0.4\text{ kN} = -400\text{ N}$

3
Construct the bending moment diagram $M(x)$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Integrate $V(x)$, and apply the jump at the concentrated moment

Since $dM/dx = V$, $M(x)$ is linear on each segment with slope equal to the local shear. $M = 0$ at both simple supports A and B.

① From A to the load ($0 \le x \le 1$):

$$M(x) = R_A\,x = 3.6x$$ $$M(1) = 3.6\text{ kN}\cdot\text{m} \quad \text{(local peak, value just before the load)}$$

② From the load to the applied moment ($1 \le x \le 2$):

$$M(x) = R_A x - 4(x-1) = -0.4x + 4$$ $$M(2^-) = -0.4(2) + 4 = 3.2\text{ kN}\cdot\text{m}$$

At mid-span, $x = 1.5$ m, which falls in this segment:

$$M(1.5) = -0.4(1.5) + 4 = 3.4\text{ kN}\cdot\text{m} = 3400\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$$

③ The concentrated moment jump at $x = 2$ m:

A concentrated applied moment causes $M(x)$ to jump by its magnitude at that point (opposite in sense to the applied moment's rotation), while $V(x)$ is unaffected:

$$M(2^+) = M(2^-) - 2.8 = 3.2 - 2.8 = 0.4\text{ kN}\cdot\text{m}$$

④ From the applied moment to B ($2 \le x \le 3$):

$$M(x) = -0.4x + 4 - 2.8 = -0.4x + 1.2$$ $$M(3) = -0.4(3) + 1.2 = 0 \quad \checkmark \text{ (zero at the roller, as required)}$$

Bending moment at mid-span ($x = 1.5$ m): $M = 3.4\text{ kN}\cdot\text{m} = 3400\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$

Maximum bending moment along the whole beam is $M_{max} = 3.6\text{ kN}\cdot\text{m}$, occurring directly under the 4 kN load at $x = 1$ m — this, not the mid-span value, is the critical section for design.

4
Summary
Step 4 Beam, shear force diagram, and bending moment diagram — aligned

$V$ at mid-span ($x=1.5$ m) $= -400$ N     $M$ at mid-span ($x=1.5$ m) $= 3400\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$

Common mistake: Students often forget that a concentrated applied moment causes a jump in the bending moment diagram but not in the shear force diagram — only point forces (and support reactions) create shear jumps. Skipping the $M(2^-) \to M(2^+)$ discontinuity, or applying it with the wrong sign, is the most frequent error in this type of problem.
