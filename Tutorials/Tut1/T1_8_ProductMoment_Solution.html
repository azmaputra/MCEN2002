Tutorial 1.8  –  Product Moment of Area: Four Shapes

Problem Statement

Determine the product moment of area $I_{xy}$ for each of the shaded areas about the x and y axes.

Given answer:   (a) and (c) $I_{xy} = 9.60 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$    (b) $I_{xy} = -4.71 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$    (d) $I_{xy} = -2.98 \times 10^6\ \text{mm}^4$

Solution

1
Set up the method: parallel axis theorem for $I_{xy}$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Why every shape here reduces to a single transfer term

The parallel axis theorem for the product moment of area is:

$$I_{xy} = \bar{I}_{xy} + A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y}$$

where $\bar{I}_{xy}$ is the product moment about the shape's own centroidal axes, and $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ is the shape's centroid measured from the x–y axes.

Key observation: every shape in this problem — rectangle, circle, and semicircle — has at least one axis of symmetry parallel to x or y. Any such axis of symmetry makes $\bar{I}_{xy} = 0$. So for all four shapes:

$$I_{xy} = A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y}$$

The whole problem therefore comes down to two things: the area of each shape, and the signed coordinates of its centroid. The sign of $I_{xy}$ follows the quadrant: positive in the 1st and 3rd quadrants ($\bar{x}\bar{y} > 0$), negative in the 2nd and 4th ($\bar{x}\bar{y} < 0$).

2
Shape (a): rectangle in the first quadrant
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Rectangle 80 × 40 mm, centroid at (60, 50)

The rectangle is 80 mm wide and 40 mm tall. From the dimensions, its centroid lies 60 mm right of the y-axis and 50 mm above the x-axis:

$$A = 80 \times 40 = 3\,200\text{ mm}^2 \qquad \bar{x} = +60\text{ mm}, \quad \bar{y} = +50\text{ mm}$$
$$I_{xy} = A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y} = 3\,200 \times 60 \times 50 = 9\,600\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

$$I_{xy}^{(a)} = +9.60 \times 10^6\text{ mm}^4$$

Sign check: the area sits entirely in the 1st quadrant ($\bar{x} > 0$, $\bar{y} > 0$), so $I_{xy}$ must be positive. ✓

3
Shape (b): circle in the second quadrant
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Circle r = 25 mm, centre at (−60, 40)

A circle's centroid is its centre. With radius 25 mm, centre 60 mm left of the y-axis and 40 mm above the x-axis:

$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi (25)^2 = 1\,963.5\text{ mm}^2 \qquad \bar{x} = -60\text{ mm}, \quad \bar{y} = +40\text{ mm}$$
$$I_{xy} = A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y} = 1\,963.5 \times (-60) \times 40 = -4\,712\,400\text{ mm}^4$$

$$I_{xy}^{(b)} = -4.71 \times 10^6\text{ mm}^4$$

Sign check: 2nd quadrant ($\bar{x} < 0$, $\bar{y} > 0$) → negative. ✓ Note how sensitive $I_{xy}$ is to the centroid position: if the centre were at $\bar{y} = 50$ mm instead of 40 mm, the result would change to $-5.89 \times 10^6$ mm⁴ — a 25% difference from a 10 mm shift. Always read the datum of each dimension carefully.

4
Shape (c): rectangle in the third quadrant
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Rectangle 50 × 80 mm, centroid at (−60, −40)

This rectangle is 50 mm wide and 80 mm tall, with its top edge on the x-axis and its vertical centreline shared with circle (b) at $x = -60$ mm. Its centroid is therefore 40 mm below the x-axis:

$$A = 50 \times 80 = 4\,000\text{ mm}^2 \qquad \bar{x} = -60\text{ mm}, \quad \bar{y} = -40\text{ mm}$$
$$I_{xy} = A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y} = 4\,000 \times (-60) \times (-40) = 9\,600\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

$$I_{xy}^{(c)} = +9.60 \times 10^6\text{ mm}^4$$

Note: (a) and (c) give the same value even though the shapes differ — a coincidence of $A\bar{x}\bar{y}$: $3200 \times 3000 = 4000 \times 2400$. And in the 3rd quadrant the two negative coordinates multiply to give a positive product, just like the 1st quadrant.

5
Shape (d): semicircle in the fourth quadrant
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 5 Semicircle r = 25 mm — don't forget the 4r/3π centroid offset

The semicircle has radius 25 mm with its flat edge facing up, 40 mm below the x-axis, and bulging downward. Its diameter midpoint is on the line $x = +60$ mm.

A semicircle's centroid does not lie on the flat edge — it sits a distance $\dfrac{4r}{3\pi}$ from the flat edge, toward the curved side:

$$\frac{4r}{3\pi} = \frac{4 \times 25}{3\pi} = 10.61\text{ mm}$$

Since the shape bulges downward, the centroid sits 10.61 mm below the flat edge:

$$\bar{y} = -(40 + 10.61) = -50.61\text{ mm} \qquad \bar{x} = +60\text{ mm}$$

The semicircle is symmetric about the vertical line through its centroid, so $\bar{I}_{xy} = 0$ and only the transfer term remains:

$$A = \frac{\pi r^2}{2} = \frac{\pi (25)^2}{2} = 981.7\text{ mm}^2$$
$$I_{xy} = A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y} = 981.7 \times 60 \times (-50.61) = -2\,981\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

$$I_{xy}^{(d)} = -2.98 \times 10^6\text{ mm}^4$$

Sign check: 4th quadrant ($\bar{x} > 0$, $\bar{y} < 0$) → negative. ✓

Common mistake 1: Assuming $I_{xy} = 0$ "because the shape is symmetric." Symmetry makes only the centroidal $\bar{I}_{xy}$ zero. About axes that don't pass through the centroid, the transfer term $A\bar{x}\bar{y}$ is generally non-zero — here it is the entire answer for every shape.

Common mistake 2: Dropping the signs of $\bar{x}$ and $\bar{y}$. Unlike $I_x$ and $I_y$ (always positive), $I_{xy}$ can be negative — the quadrant of the centroid sets the sign.

Common mistake 3: For the semicircle, using the flat-edge distance (40 mm) as $\bar{y}$ and forgetting the $4r/3\pi = 10.61$ mm centroid offset. That error would give $-2.36 \times 10^6$ instead of $-2.98 \times 10^6$ mm⁴.
