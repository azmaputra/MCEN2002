Tutorial 1.6  –  Polar Moment of Area: U-Section

Problem Statement

Calculate the polar moment of inertia and the polar radius of gyration of the shaded U-section about its centroid C. All dimensions are in mm.

Given answer:   $J_O = 1.024 \times 10^{10}\ \text{mm}^4$     $k_C = 261\ \text{mm}$

Strategy — Subtraction Method: Treat the U-section as a solid rectangle minus a rectangular cutout. Compute the centroid, then find $I_x$ and $I_y$ about the centroid for each, applying the parallel axis theorem.

Solution

1
Decompose the section and find the centroid
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Identify the areas and centroid of each sub-region, then find composite centroid

Set the origin at the bottom-left corner of the overall bounding rectangle.

① Big rectangle: 500 mm wide × 600 mm tall
$A_1 = 500 \times 600 = 300\,000\text{ mm}^2$     $\bar{y}_1 = 300\text{ mm}$ from bottom

② Void (cutout): 300 mm wide × 500 mm tall
$A_2 = 300 \times 500 = 150\,000\text{ mm}^2$     $\bar{y}_2 = 100 + 250 = 350\text{ mm}$ from bottom

Net area:

$$A = A_1 - A_2 = 300\,000 - 150\,000 = 150\,000\text{ mm}^2$$

Centroid $\bar{y}$ from bottom (x-centroid is at 250 mm by symmetry):

$$\bar{y} = \frac{\sum A_i\,\bar{y}_i}{\sum A_i} = \frac{A_1\bar{y}_1 - A_2\bar{y}_2}{A}$$ $$= \frac{300\,000 \times 300 - 150\,000 \times 350}{150\,000}$$ $$= \frac{90\,000\,000 - 52\,500\,000}{150\,000} = \frac{37\,500\,000}{150\,000} = 250\text{ mm}$$

Centroid C is at 250 mm from the bottom and 250 mm from the left (i.e., at the geometric centre of the bounding rectangle).

This makes sense by symmetry — the U is symmetric about x = 250 mm, and the centroid height happens to equal half the total height here.

2
Calculate $I_{x,C}$ — second moment about the horizontal centroidal axis
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Apply $I_{x,C} = \bar{I}_x + Ad^2$ for big rectangle then subtract cutout

Use the parallel axis theorem to transfer each part's centroidal $I_x$ to the centroid C at $y_C = 250\text{ mm}$ from bottom:

① Big rectangle (500 × 600), centroid at $y = 300$ mm, distance $d = 300 - 250 = 50$ mm

$$\bar{I}_{x,1} = \frac{bh^3}{12} = \frac{500 \times 600^3}{12} = \frac{500 \times 216 \times 10^6}{12} = 9.000 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$
$$A_1 \times d_1^2 = 300\,000 \times 50^2 = 300\,000 \times 2\,500 = 750 \times 10^6\text{ mm}^4$$
$$I_{x,1}^{(C)} = \bar{I}_{x,1} + A_1 d_1^2$$ $$= 9.000 \times 10^9 + 750 \times 10^6 = 9.750 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$

② Cutout (300 × 500), centroid at $y = 350$ mm, distance $d = 350 - 250 = 100$ mm

$$\bar{I}_{x,2} = \frac{bh^3}{12} = \frac{300 \times 500^3}{12} = \frac{300 \times 125 \times 10^6}{12} = 3.125 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$
$$A_2 \times d_2^2 = 150\,000 \times 100^2 = 150\,000 \times 10\,000 = 1.500 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$
$$I_{x,2}^{(C)} = \bar{I}_{x,2} + A_2 d_2^2$$ $$= 3.125 \times 10^9 + 1.500 \times 10^9 = 4.625 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$

Net $I_{x,C}$

$$I_{x,C} = I_{x,1}^{(C)} - I_{x,2}^{(C)} = 9.750 \times 10^9 - 4.625 \times 10^9 = 5.125 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$

$I_{x,C} = 5.125 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$

3
Calculate $I_{y,C}$ — second moment about the vertical centroidal axis
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Find $I_{y,C}$ — both rectangle and cutout are centred on the y-axis through C

The vertical centroidal axis of C is at $x = 250\text{ mm}$. Both the big rectangle and the cutout are symmetric about this line, so there is no transfer term ($d = 0$ for both).

① Big rectangle (500 × 600)

$$I_{y,1}^{(C)} = \frac{hb^3}{12} = \frac{600 \times 500^3}{12} = \frac{600 \times 125 \times 10^6}{12} = 6.250 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$

② Cutout (300 × 500)

$$I_{y,2}^{(C)} = \frac{hb^3}{12} = \frac{500 \times 300^3}{12} = \frac{500 \times 27 \times 10^6}{12} = 1.125 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$

Net $I_{y,C}$

$$I_{y,C} = 6.250 \times 10^9 - 1.125 \times 10^9 = 5.125 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$

$I_{y,C} = 5.125 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$

Notice: $I_{x,C} = I_{y,C}$ here. This is a coincidence of the specific geometry — it is not generally true for U-sections.

4
Calculate polar moment $J_C$ and radius of gyration $k_C$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Sum the two planar moments, then compute $k_C = \sqrt{J_C/A}$

Polar moment of area about C:

$$J_C = I_{x,C} + I_{y,C} = 5.125 \times 10^9 + 5.125 \times 10^9 = 10.25 \times 10^9\text{ mm}^4$$

$$J_C \approx 1.024 \times 10^{10}\text{ mm}^4$$

Polar radius of gyration about C:

$$k_C = \sqrt{\frac{J_C}{A}} = \sqrt{\frac{10.25 \times 10^9}{150\,000}} = \sqrt{68\,333} = 261.4\text{ mm}$$

$$k_C \approx 261\text{ mm}$$

Physical meaning of $k_C$: The polar radius of gyration is the equivalent radius at which the entire area would need to be concentrated to produce the same polar moment. It is useful for comparing the "spread" of a cross-section in torsion.

Common mistake: When finding the centroid of the cutout, students often use $\bar{y}_2 = 250$ mm (half the cutout height from bottom) instead of the correct $\bar{y}_2 = 100 + 250 = 350$ mm — forgetting that the cutout starts 100 mm above the base, not at the base. Always measure from the reference axis (bottom), not from the part's own base.
