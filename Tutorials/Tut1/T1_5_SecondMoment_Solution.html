Tutorial 1.5  –  Second Moment of Area: Rectangle

Problem Statement

Calculate the second moment of area about the x and y axes, and the polar moment of area about point O.

Given answer:   $I_x = 2.106 \times 10^7\ \text{mm}^4$     $I_y = 1.458 \times 10^7\ \text{mm}^4$     $J_O = 3.564 \times 10^7\ \text{mm}^4$

Solution

1
Identify the geometry and centroid distances
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Locate the rectangle's centroid relative to O

The rectangle has width $b = 90\text{ mm}$ (extending to the left of O) and height $h = 60\text{ mm}$ (top edge is 30 mm below O).

The centroid $\bar{C}$ of the rectangle is at its geometric centre:

Distance from centroid to x-axis (horizontal through O):

$d_y = 30 + \dfrac{60}{2} = 30 + 30 = 60\text{ mm}$

Distance from centroid to y-axis (vertical through O):

$d_x = \dfrac{90}{2} = 45\text{ mm}$

These distances $d_y$ and $d_x$ will be used in the parallel axis theorem to shift the centroidal moments to axes through O.

2
Calculate $I_x$ about the x-axis through O
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Apply the parallel axis theorem to find $I_x$

The second moment of area about the x-axis through O uses the parallel axis theorem:

$$I_x = \bar{I}_x + A \, d_y^2$$

where $\bar{I}_x = \dfrac{bh^3}{12}$ is the centroidal second moment of area about a horizontal axis.

Centroidal second moment:

$$\bar{I}_x = \frac{bh^3}{12} = \frac{90 \times 60^3}{12} = \frac{90 \times 216\,000}{12} = 1\,620\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

Area and transfer term:

$$A = b \times h = 90 \times 60 = 5\,400\text{ mm}^2$$ $$A\,d_y^2 = 5\,400 \times 60^2 = 5\,400 \times 3\,600 = 19\,440\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

Total:

$$I_x = 1\,620\,000 + 19\,440\,000 = 21\,060\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

$$I_x = 2.106 \times 10^7\text{ mm}^4$$

Note: The transfer term $A\,d_y^2$ accounts for the fact that the x-axis is 60 mm away from the rectangle's centroid — this dominates over the centroidal term.

3
Calculate $I_y$ about the y-axis through O
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Apply the parallel axis theorem to find $I_y$

The second moment of area about the y-axis through O:

$$I_y = \bar{I}_y + A \, d_x^2$$

where $\bar{I}_y = \dfrac{hb^3}{12}$ is the centroidal second moment of area about a vertical axis.

Centroidal second moment:

$$\bar{I}_y = \frac{h b^3}{12} = \frac{60 \times 90^3}{12} = \frac{60 \times 729\,000}{12} = 3\,645\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

Transfer term:

$$A\,d_x^2 = 5\,400 \times 45^2 = 5\,400 \times 2\,025 = 10\,935\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

Total:

$$I_y = 3\,645\,000 + 10\,935\,000 = 14\,580\,000\text{ mm}^4$$

$$I_y = 1.458 \times 10^7\text{ mm}^4$$

4
Calculate the polar moment $J_O$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Sum $I_x$ and $I_y$ to get the polar moment about O

The polar moment of area (also written $J_O$ or $I_p$) about a point is the sum of the two orthogonal second moments of area:

$$J_O = I_x + I_y$$
$$J_O = 2.106 \times 10^7 + 1.458 \times 10^7 = 3.564 \times 10^7\text{ mm}^4$$

$$J_O = 3.564 \times 10^7\text{ mm}^4$$

Physical meaning: $J_O$ measures the section's resistance to twisting (torsion) about point O. It is always the sum of the two planar second moments, regardless of axis orientation.

Common mistake: Using $d_y = 30\text{ mm}$ (top-edge distance) instead of $d_y = 60\text{ mm}$ (centroid distance). The parallel axis theorem always uses the distance from the centroidal axis to the target axis — not from a corner or edge.
