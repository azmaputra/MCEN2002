Tutorial 1.10  –  Principal Moments of Inertia: Composite Z-Section

Problem Statement

Determine the principal moments of inertia with respect to the centroidal axes through C of the composite shaded area. Find the angle α measured from the x axis to the axis of maximum moment of inertia.

Given answer:   $I_{min} = 0.505\,a^4$     $I_{max} = 6.165\,a^4$     $\alpha = 112.5°$

Solution

1
Confirm the centroid and set up the composite geometry
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Locate each rectangle's centroid relative to C

The shaded area consists of two identical rectangles, each of width $2a$ and height $a$, with area:

$$A_1 = A_2 = 2a \times a = 2a^2 \qquad A_{total} = 4a^2$$

Measuring from C, the rectangle centroids are located at:

Rectangle 1 (upper right):   $\bar{x}_1 = +a$,   $\bar{y}_1 = +\dfrac{a}{2}$

Rectangle 2 (lower left):   $\bar{x}_2 = -a$,   $\bar{y}_2 = -\dfrac{a}{2}$

Because the two centroids are equal and opposite ($A_1\bar{x}_1 + A_2\bar{x}_2 = 0$ and likewise for $y$), the shape is point-symmetric about C — confirming that C is indeed the centroid of the composite area. No centroid calculation is needed; we can apply the parallel axis theorem directly about the x–y axes through C.

2
Calculate $I_x$ and $I_y$ about the centroidal axes
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Apply the parallel axis theorem to each rectangle

By symmetry the two rectangles contribute identically, so compute one and double it.

About the x-axis (rectangle: $b = 2a$, $h = a$, transfer distance $d_y = a/2$):

$$I_x^{(1)} = \frac{b h^3}{12} + A\,d_y^2 = \frac{(2a)(a)^3}{12} + 2a^2\left(\frac{a}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{a^4}{6} + \frac{a^4}{2} = \frac{2a^4}{3}$$
$$I_x = 2 \times \frac{2a^4}{3} = \frac{4a^4}{3} = 1.333\,a^4$$

About the y-axis (transfer distance $d_x = a$):

$$I_y^{(1)} = \frac{h b^3}{12} + A\,d_x^2 = \frac{(a)(2a)^3}{12} + 2a^2(a)^2 = \frac{2a^4}{3} + 2a^4 = \frac{8a^4}{3}$$
$$I_y = 2 \times \frac{8a^4}{3} = \frac{16a^4}{3} = 5.333\,a^4$$

$$I_x = 1.333\,a^4 \qquad I_y = 5.333\,a^4$$

Sanity check: $I_y > I_x$ makes physical sense — the area is spread much further horizontally (total span $4a$) than vertically (total span $2a$).

3
Calculate the product of inertia $I_{xy}$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Apply the parallel axis theorem for the product of inertia

For each rectangle, the product of inertia about its own centroidal axes is zero (a rectangle has two axes of symmetry), so only the transfer term remains:

$$I_{xy} = \sum \left( \bar{I}_{xy} + A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y} \right) = \sum A\,\bar{x}\,\bar{y}$$

Rectangle 1:

$$I_{xy}^{(1)} = 2a^2 \times (+a) \times \left(+\frac{a}{2}\right) = +a^4$$

Rectangle 2:

$$I_{xy}^{(2)} = 2a^2 \times (-a) \times \left(-\frac{a}{2}\right) = +a^4$$

$$I_{xy} = a^4 + a^4 = 2\,a^4$$

Note the signs: both rectangles sit in quadrants where $\bar{x}$ and $\bar{y}$ have the same sign (1st and 3rd quadrants), so both contributions are positive — they add rather than cancel. A non-zero $I_{xy}$ is exactly why the x–y axes are not the principal axes for this Z-shaped section.

4
Calculate the principal moments of inertia
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Use the principal moment (Mohr's circle) equations

The principal moments of inertia are the centre of Mohr's circle plus/minus its radius:

$$I_{max,\,min} = \frac{I_x + I_y}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{I_x - I_y}{2}\right)^2 + I_{xy}^2}$$

Centre (average):

$$\frac{I_x + I_y}{2} = \frac{1.333 + 5.333}{2}\,a^4 = \frac{10}{3}\,a^4 = 3.333\,a^4$$

Radius:

$$R = \sqrt{\left(\frac{1.333 - 5.333}{2}\right)^2 + (2)^2}\;a^4 = \sqrt{(-2)^2 + 2^2}\;a^4 = 2\sqrt{2}\,a^4 = 2.828\,a^4$$

Principal moments:

$$I_{max} = (3.333 + 2.828)\,a^4 = 6.162\,a^4 \qquad I_{min} = (3.333 - 2.828)\,a^4 = 0.505\,a^4$$

$$I_{max} = 6.16\,a^4 \qquad I_{min} = 0.505\,a^4$$

Check: $I_{max} + I_{min} = I_x + I_y = 6.667\,a^4$ — the polar moment about C is invariant under axis rotation. (The quoted answer of $6.165\,a^4$ carries a small rounding error; the exact value is $\frac{10}{3} + 2\sqrt{2} = 6.162\,a^4$.)

5
Find the angle α to the axis of maximum moment of inertia
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 5 Solve for the principal angle and identify which root is the maximum

The orientation of the principal axes follows from:

$$\tan 2\theta_p = \frac{-2 I_{xy}}{I_x - I_y} = \frac{-2(2)}{1.333 - 5.333} = \frac{-4}{-4} = 1$$
$$2\theta_p = 45° \;\;\text{or}\;\; 225° \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \theta_p = 22.5° \;\;\text{or}\;\; 112.5°$$

The two roots are 90° apart — one gives $I_{max}$, the other $I_{min}$. You must check which is which. Substitute $\theta = 112.5°$ into the transformation equation:

$$I_{\theta} = \frac{I_x + I_y}{2} + \frac{I_x - I_y}{2}\cos 2\theta - I_{xy}\sin 2\theta$$
$$I_{112.5°} = 3.333 + (-2)(\cos 225°) - 2(\sin 225°)$$ $$= 3.333 + 1.414 + 1.414 = 6.16\,a^4 = I_{max} \; ✓$$

(Substituting $\theta = 22.5°$ gives $0.505\,a^4 = I_{min}$.)

$$\alpha = 112.5° \;\; \text{(from the x-axis to the axis of } I_{max})$$

Physical intuition: the axis of $I_{max}$ should be roughly perpendicular to the direction along which the area is stretched out. The Z-section stretches from lower-left to upper-right (≈ 22.5° zone), so the material lies far from an axis at 112.5° — giving the largest moment of inertia. Since $I_{xy} > 0$, the maximum axis is always found rotating from the axis of the larger of $I_x, I_y$ (here the y-axis) toward the negative $I_{xy}$ direction.

Common mistake 1: Forgetting the transfer term $A\bar{x}\bar{y}$ for the product of inertia, or assuming $I_{xy}=0$ "because each rectangle is symmetric." Symmetry of each part makes only its centroidal $\bar{I}_{xy}$ zero — the transfer terms still contribute, and here they are the entire $I_{xy}$.

Common mistake 2: Reporting $\theta_p = 22.5°$ as the answer. $\tan 2\theta_p$ gives two roots 90° apart; always substitute back (or use Mohr's circle) to determine which root corresponds to $I_{max}$. Here 22.5° is the minimum axis.
