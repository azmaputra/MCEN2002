Tutorial 1.3  –  Rotating Disk Balance

Problem Statement

The circular disk rotates about an axis (out of the page) through its centre O. It has two holes of diameter $d$, both at radius $r$ from the centre: one on the negative $x$-axis and one on the negative $y$-axis. A third hole of diameter $D$ is to be drilled at the same radius $r$ at an angle $\theta$ from the positive $x$-axis (measured anti-clockwise), so that the disk is in balance — that is, the mass centre remains at $O(0,\ 0)$.

Determine $D$ (in terms of $d$) and the angular position $\theta$ of the third hole.

Given answer:   $\theta = 45°$    $D = \sqrt[4]{2}\;d \approx 1.189\,d$

Key Principle: Balance Condition

Recall the composite centroid formulae — the centroid of a body made up of parts is:

$$\bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum A_i\,\bar{x}_i}{\sum A_i} \qquad \bar{y} = \dfrac{\sum A_i\,\bar{y}_i}{\sum A_i}$$

For the disk to be balanced, we force the centroid to sit at the origin: $\bar{x} = 0$ and $\bar{y} = 0$. Since the total area $\sum A_i \neq 0$, the only way these fractions can equal zero is if the numerators are zero:

$$\sum A_i\,\bar{x}_i = 0 \qquad \sum A_i\,\bar{y}_i = 0$$

These are the two equations we solve. Each hole area $A_i$ is positive; the sign of its centroid coordinate $(\bar{x}_i,\,\bar{y}_i)$ carries the directional information.

1
Identify Areas and Centroid Positions of Each Hole
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 1 Show solution

All three holes lie at radius $r$ from O. Let the third hole be at angle $\theta$ from the positive $x$-axis (anti-clockwise). The centroid of each hole coincides with its centre.

Hole Area $A_i$ Centre $\bar{x}_i$ Centre $\bar{y}_i$
① Hole 1  (diameter $d$, on $-x$ axis) $\dfrac{\pi d^2}{4}$ $-r$ $0$
② Hole 2  (diameter $d$, on $-y$ axis) $\dfrac{\pi d^2}{4}$ $0$ $-r$
③ Hole 3  (diameter $D$, at angle $\theta$) $\dfrac{\pi D^2}{4}$ $r\cos\theta$ $r\sin\theta$
2
Apply the Balance Condition (x and y equations)
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 2 Show solution

Substitute into $\sum A_i \bar{x}_i = 0$ and $\sum A_i \bar{y}_i = 0$. The factor $\dfrac{\pi r}{4}$ cancels throughout.

x-equation:

$$\frac{\pi d^2}{4}(-r) + \frac{\pi d^2}{4}(0) + \frac{\pi D^2}{4}(r\cos\theta) = 0$$ $$-d^2 + D^2\cos\theta = 0$$ $$\boxed{D^2\cos\theta = d^2} \tag{1}$$

y-equation:

$$\frac{\pi d^2}{4}(0) + \frac{\pi d^2}{4}(-r) + \frac{\pi D^2}{4}(r\sin\theta) = 0$$ $$-d^2 + D^2\sin\theta = 0$$ $$\boxed{D^2\sin\theta = d^2} \tag{2}$$

Notice that equations (1) and (2) have identical right-hand sides — this means $D^2\cos\theta = D^2\sin\theta$, which immediately tells us something about $\theta$.

3
Solve for the Angle $\theta$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 3 Show solution

Divide equation (2) by equation (1):

$$\frac{D^2\sin\theta}{D^2\cos\theta} = \frac{d^2}{d^2}$$ $$\tan\theta = 1$$ $$\therefore \quad \theta = 45°$$

The third hole is positioned at 45° above the positive $x$-axis (in the first quadrant), which is consistent with the figure.

4
Solve for the Diameter $D$
Try it first, then reveal ↓
Step 4 Show solution

Substitute $\theta = 45°$ into equation (1):

$$D^2 \cos 45° = d^2$$ $$D^2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = d^2$$ $$D^2 = d^2\sqrt{2}$$ $$D = d \cdot \left(\sqrt{2}\right)^{1/2} = d \cdot 2^{1/4}$$ $$\therefore \quad D = \sqrt[4]{2}\; d \approx 1.189\, d$$

Verification using equation (2):

$$D^2\sin 45° = d^2\sqrt{2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = d^2 \checkmark$$

Answer:

$\theta = 45°$

$D = \sqrt[4]{2}\;d = 2^{1/4}\,d \approx 1.189\,d$

Physical interpretation: A single hole of diameter $D = \sqrt[4]{2}\,d \approx 1.19d$ at $\theta = 45°$ has exactly enough area at exactly the right angular position to counterbalance the combined effect of the two smaller holes. Note that $D > d$ because one hole must compensate for the combined pull of two holes — but the 45° placement means the single hole contributes equally to both the $x$ and $y$ balance.

Summary of Results

Angular Position
$\theta = 45°$
Hole Diameter
$D = \sqrt[4]{2}\;d \approx 1.189\,d$
