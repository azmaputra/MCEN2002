Parallel Axis Theorem

Shifting the second moment of area from a shape's own centroid to any parallel axis — the tool that makes composite sections tractable.

Section 1.1 First Moment & Centroid Section 1.2 Second Moment of Area Section 1.3 Parallel Axis Theorem Section 1.4 Polar Moment Section 1.5 Summary
⬅️ Where you left off

In Section 1.2 you derived \(I_x\) for a rectangle, triangle, and circle — always about that shape's own centroidal axis. But real cross-sections — I-beams, T-sections, angle brackets — are built from several simple shapes stacked together, and only one of them (at most) has its centroid sitting on the composite section's neutral axis. Every other piece is offset. You need a way to correct \(I_x\) for that offset without re-integrating from scratch every time. That's the Parallel Axis Theorem.

Core Concept

Why offset increases \(I_x\) — and by how much

🎯 Why this concept

Standard formulas give \(I_x\) about a shape's own centroid. In a composite section, each sub-component's centroid sits away from the composite centroid, at some distance \(d\). The Parallel Axis Theorem tells you exactly how much extra \(I_x\) that offset contributes — \(Ad^2\) — so you can build up the total \(I_x\) of a composite section piece by piece. This is the calculation you'll use constantly once you get to beam design.

If a sub-component's centroid is at a perpendicular distance \(d\) from the axis you actually care about, every area element in that sub-component is now \(d\) further from that axis than it was from its own centroid. Because \(I_x\) is a squared-distance integral, that offset doesn't just shift \(I_x\) a little — it can dominate it. This is the mathematical reason an I-beam's flanges, sitting far from the neutral axis, do most of the structural work.

Parallel Axis Theorem
\[I_x = \bar{I}_x + Ad^2\]
\(I_x\)Second moment of area about the target \(x\)-axis (usually the composite centroidal axis)
\(\bar{I}_x\)Second moment of area about the sub-component's own centroidal axis, parallel to the target axis — from the standard formulas in Section 1.2
\(A\)Area of the sub-component
\(d\)Perpendicular distance between the sub-component's centroidal axis and the target axis
⚠️ Most common mistake: applying \(Ad^2\) before finding the composite centroid. \(d\) is measured from each sub-component's centroid to the composite centroidal axis — never to an arbitrary reference axis, and never before you know where the composite centroid actually is. Find the composite centroid first (Section 1.1), every time.

Where does \(I_x = \bar I_x + Ad^2\) come from?

The formula isn't an approximation — it falls straight out of the definition of \(I_x\) once you split the distance into two parts. The derivation is short and worth seeing once.

Step-by-step

Procedure for a Composite Section

Seven steps, worked through on a single composite shape across four pages — each page builds on the last.

  • Step 1: Divide the cross-section into simple sub-components (rectangles, circles, etc.)
  • Step 2: Find the centroid of each sub-component from its geometry
  • Step 3: Find the composite centroid \(\bar y\) using the weighted-average formula from Section 1.1
  • Step 4: For each sub-component, calculate \(\bar I_x\) about its own centroid using the standard formulas from Section 1.2
  • Step 5: For each sub-component, find \(d\) = distance between its centroid and the composite centroid
  • Step 6: Apply \(I_x = \bar I_x + Ad^2\) for each sub-component
  • Step 7: Sum all contributions: \(I_{x,centroid} = \sum(\bar I_x + Ad^2)\)

Worked Example

Rectangle about its base, not its centroid

A rectangle has width \(b = 80\text{ mm}\) and height \(h = 150\text{ mm}\). Find \(I_x\) about an axis along its base, rather than through its centroid.

\[\bar I_x = \frac{bh^3}{12} = \frac{80 \times 150^3}{12} = 22.5 \times 10^6 \text{ mm}^4\]

The centroid sits \(h/2 = 75\text{ mm}\) above the base, so \(d = 75\text{ mm}\) and \(A = bh = 12{,}000\text{ mm}^2\):

\[\begin{aligned} I_{x,base} &= \bar I_x + Ad^2 \\ &= 22.5\times10^6 + (12{,}000)(75)^2 \\ &= 22.5\times10^6 + 67.5\times10^6 \\ &= 90\times10^6 \text{ mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]

Notice the offset term is three times larger than \(\bar I_x\) itself — the base axis result is \(bh^3/3\), the standard formula for a rectangle about its edge, and you can see exactly where that factor of 4 (12 → 3) comes from.

Worked Example · Composite Section

T-section: total \(I_x\) about the base

Split the T into a flange and a web, find the composite centroid measured from the base, then apply the parallel axis theorem to each piece before summing — the same procedure as the box above, worked through on real numbers.

Page 1 · The problem

A T-section is formed from a flange (\(80\text{ mm} \times 20\text{ mm}\)) sitting on top of a web (\(40\text{ mm} \times 60\text{ mm}\)), the web centered under the flange. Find \(I_x\), the total second moment of area, using the base of the section as the reference line for measuring centroid positions.

Page 2 · Finding the centroid

Split into two rectangles and locate each one's own centroid, measured up from the base:

\[A_1 = 80 \times 20 = 1600\text{ mm}^2 \qquad y_1 = 70\text{ mm}\] \[A_2 = 40 \times 60 = 2400\text{ mm}^2 \qquad y_2 = 30\text{ mm}\]

Weighted average gives the composite centroid, still measured from the base:

\[\bar y = \frac{A_1y_1 + A_2y_2}{A_1+A_2} = \frac{1600(70) + 2400(30)}{4000} = 46\text{ mm}\]
Page 3 · Calculate \(d\) and \(I_{total}\)

Distance from each component's own centroid to the composite centroidal axis:

\[d_1 = |70 - 46| = 24\text{ mm}\] \[d_2 = |46 - 30| = 16\text{ mm}\]

Each component's own \(\bar I_x\), then shifted to the composite centroidal axis:

\[\bar I_{x,1} = \frac{80(20)^3}{12} = 53{,}333\text{ mm}^4\] \[I_{x,1} = 53{,}333 + 1600(24)^2 = 974{,}933\text{ mm}^4\] \[\bar I_{x,2} = \frac{40(60)^3}{12} = 720{,}000\text{ mm}^4\] \[I_{x,2} = 720{,}000 + 2400(16)^2 = 1{,}334{,}400\text{ mm}^4\]

Sum to get \(I_x\) about the composite centroidal axis, then apply the parallel axis theorem once more to shift from that centroidal axis down to the base — using \(A = 4000\text{ mm}^2\) and \(\bar y = 46\text{ mm}\):

\[\begin{aligned} I_{x,centroid} &= 974{,}933 + 1{,}334{,}400 \\ &= 2{,}309{,}333\text{ mm}^4 \end{aligned}\] \[\begin{aligned} I_{x,base} &= I_{x,centroid} + A\bar y^2 \\ &= 2{,}309{,}333 + 4000(46)^2 \\ &= 10{,}773{,}333\text{ mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]

Worked Example · Composite Section

Rectangle with a half-circular end: total \(I_y\) about the left edge

This time the composite is built the other way round: a \(100\text{ mm} \times 80\text{ mm}\) rectangle with a semicircular end (radius \(40\text{ mm}\)) attached flush to its right-hand side. Because the two pieces sit side by side along \(x\) rather than stacked along \(y\), you're finding \(I_y\) — the same parallel axis logic, just rotated \(90°\).

Page 1 · The problem

A \(100\text{ mm} \times 80\text{ mm}\) rectangle has a semicircular end of radius \(40\text{ mm}\) attached to its right side, with the semicircle's flat (diameter) edge flush against the rectangle's right edge. Find \(I_y\), the total second moment of area, using the rectangle's left edge as the reference line for measuring centroid positions.

Page 2 · Finding the centroid

Split into a rectangle and a semicircle, and locate each one's own centroid, measured from the left edge. The rectangle's centroid is just half its width, but the semicircle needs a standard result: its centroid sits a distance \(\dfrac{4r}{3\pi}\) from its flat (diametral) edge, along the axis of symmetry — a result worth recalling since, unlike the rectangle, it isn't obvious by inspection.

\[\bar x_{semicircle} = \frac{4r}{3\pi} \quad \text{(measured from the flat edge)}\]
\[A_1 = 100 \times 80 = 8000\text{ mm}^2 \qquad x_1 = 50\text{ mm}\] \[A_2 = \frac{\pi r^2}{2} = \frac{\pi (40)^2}{2} \approx 2513\text{ mm}^2 \qquad x_2 = 100 + \frac{4r}{3\pi} \approx 117\text{ mm}\]

Weighted average gives the composite centroid, still measured from the left edge:

\[\bar x = \frac{A_1x_1 + A_2x_2}{A_1+A_2} = \frac{8000(50) + 2513(117)}{10{,}513} \approx 66\text{ mm}\]
Page 3 · Calculate \(d\) and \(I_{total}\)

Distance from each component's own centroid to the composite centroidal axis:

\[d_1 = |50 - 66| = 16\text{ mm}\] \[d_2 = |117 - 66| = 51\text{ mm}\]

Each component's own \(\bar I_y\), then shifted to the composite centroidal axis. The rectangle's own centroidal \(\bar I_y\) is the familiar \(bh^3/12\); the semicircle again needs a standard result — its own \(\bar I_y\), about the axis through its centroid parallel to the flat edge, is:

\[\bar I_{y,semicircle} = \left(\frac{\pi}{8} - \frac{8}{9\pi}\right)r^4\]
\[\bar I_{y,1} = \frac{80(100)^3}{12} \approx 6{,}666{,}667\text{ mm}^4\] \[I_{y,1} = 6{,}666{,}667 + 8000(16)^2 \approx 8{,}714{,}667\text{ mm}^4\] \[\bar I_{y,2} = \left(\frac{\pi}{8} - \frac{8}{9\pi}\right)(40)^4 \approx 280{,}978\text{ mm}^4\] \[I_{y,2} = 280{,}978 + 2513(51)^2 \approx 6{,}817{,}991\text{ mm}^4\]

Sum to get \(I_y\) about the composite centroidal axis, then apply the parallel axis theorem once more to shift from that centroidal axis over to the left edge — using \(A = 10{,}513\text{ mm}^2\) and \(\bar x = 66\text{ mm}\):

\[\begin{aligned} I_{y,centroid} &= 8{,}714{,}667 + 6{,}817{,}991 \\ &\approx 15{,}532{,}658\text{ mm}^4 \end{aligned}\] \[\begin{aligned} I_{y,edge} &= I_{y,centroid} + A\bar x^2 \\ &\approx 15{,}532{,}658 + 10{,}513(66)^2 \\ &\approx 61{,}300{,}000\text{ mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]
