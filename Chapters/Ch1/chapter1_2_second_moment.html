Where we left off

From centroid to bending resistance

In Section 1.1, you found the centroid — the reference point from which all bending calculations are made. Now the question is: given that reference point, how does the shape of the cross-section resist bending?

Two beams can share exactly the same centroid and the same cross-sectional area, yet one can be far stiffer in bending than the other. The difference comes entirely from how the material is distributed around the centroidal axis. The quantity that captures this is the second moment of area.

🔗 Connection to stress

The bending stress formula you will derive in Chapter 2 is \(\sigma = My/I\), where \(M\) is the applied bending moment and \(y\) is the distance from the neutral axis. The second moment of area I appears in the denominator — a larger I means lower stress for the same load. Getting I right is the foundation of every beam design calculation in this course.

Core concept

Second Moment of Area (I)

🎯 Why this matters

When a beam bends, material far from the neutral axis carries more stress and must stretch or compress more. The second moment of area, \(I\), quantifies exactly how far the area is distributed from the bending axis. A larger \(I\) means the section is stiffer and stronger in bending — which is why I-beams concentrate material in their flanges, as far from the neutral axis as possible.

Intuition: distance matters more than area

Hold a flat 30 cm ruler by its ends and try to bend it the easy way — it flexes immediately. Now rotate it 90° and try to bend it the hard way — it resists far more. The material is identical in both cases. Only its orientation has changed.

This happens because the squaring of distance in the definition \(I = \int y^2 \, dA\) gives disproportionate weight to material that is far from the neutral axis. Moving a strip of material twice as far away makes it contribute four times as much to \(I\). This is the mathematical reason why hollow sections and I-beams are so much more efficient than solid bars of equal mass.

Definition

General Definition — Second Moment of Area
\[I_x = \int y^2 \, dA\]
\(I_x\)Second moment of area about the x-axis (units: mm⁴ or m⁴)
\(y\)Perpendicular distance from the x-axis to the area element \(dA\)
\(dA\)Infinitesimal area element — \(dA = b \cdot dy\) for a horizontal strip
⚠️ Always state which axis. \(I\) is always defined about a specific axis. \(I_x\) is measured from the x-axis; \(I_y\) from the y-axis. The same cross-section has different values of \(I\) about different axes — and as you will see in the examples below, the difference can be dramatic. Always label your axis before you write down a formula.

Standard results you need to know

These formulas are derived by integrating \(\int y^2 \, dA\) over each shape. You do not need to re-derive them in problem solving, but you should understand what each term represents.

Rectangle — Second Moment of Area
\[I_x = \frac{bh^3}{12}\]

\(h\) ⊥ x-axis → \(h\) is cubed

\[I_y = \frac{hb^3}{12}\]

\(b\) ⊥ y-axis → \(b\) is cubed

\(b\)Width of rectangle (horizontal dimension)
\(h\)Height of rectangle (vertical dimension)
💡 The dimension perpendicular to the axis is always cubed.
In \(I_x = bh^3/12\), it is \(h\) that is cubed because \(h\) is the dimension measured along the y-direction (perpendicular to x). In \(I_y = hb^3/12\), it is \(b\) that is cubed. A helpful check: ask yourself which dimension measures how far the material sits from that axis? That is the one cubed. For \(I_x\), material distance from the x-axis is measured vertically — that is \(h\), so \(h\) is cubed. For \(I_y\), material distance from the y-axis is measured horizontally — that is \(b\), so \(b\) is cubed.
Triangle — Second Moment of Area
\[I_x = \frac{bh^3}{36}\]

\(h\) ⊥ x-axis → \(h\) is cubed

x-axis at h/3 from base

\[I_y = \frac{hb^3}{36}\]

\(b\) ⊥ y-axis → \(b\) is cubed

y-axis at b/3 from vertical edge

\(b\)Base width of triangle
\(h\)Height of triangle (centroid at h/3 from base)
Solid Circle — About Any Centroidal Axis (by symmetry)
\[I = \frac{\pi d^4}{64} = \frac{\pi r^4}{4}\]
\(d\)Diameter of circle

Worked examples

EXAMPLE 01 Rectangular Section — Comparing \(I_x\) and \(I_y\)

Given: A rectangular cross-section 80 mm wide × 200 mm tall. Calculate \(I_x\) (about the horizontal centroidal axis) and \(I_y\) (about the vertical centroidal axis). Compare the two values and explain the structural implication.

\(I_x\) — About horizontal centroidal axis

The dimension perpendicular to the x-axis is the height h = 200 mm.

CALCULATION
\[I_x = \frac{bh^3}{12} = \frac{80 \times 200^3}{12}\]
\[= \frac{80 \times 8{,}000{,}000}{12} = \frac{640{,}000{,}000}{12}\]
\[I_x = 53.3 \times 10^6 \text{ mm}^4\]

\(I_y\) — About vertical centroidal axis

The dimension perpendicular to the y-axis is the width b = 80 mm.

CALCULATION
\[I_y = \frac{hb^3}{12} = \frac{200 \times 80^3}{12}\]
\[= \frac{200 \times 512{,}000}{12} = \frac{102{,}400{,}000}{12}\]
\[I_y = 8.53 \times 10^6 \text{ mm}^4\]
RESULT AND INTERPRETATION

\(I_x / I_y = 53.3 / 8.53 = 6.25\)

The section is 6.25× stiffer about the x-axis than the y-axis — purely because of orientation. When this beam is used as a structural member, it should be oriented with the larger dimension (\(h\) = 200 mm) vertical, so that \(I_x\) resists vertical bending loads. This is why rectangular timber joists are always installed "the tall way up".

EXAMPLE 02 Circular Solid Section and Annulus

Given: (a) A solid circular cross-section of diameter d = 100 mm. (b) A hollow circular section (annulus) with outer diameter do = 100 mm and inner diameter di = 60 mm. Calculate I for both sections and compare. Both sections carry the same outer profile.

(a) Solid circle — \(d\) = 100 mm

CALCULATION
\[I = \frac{\pi d^4}{64} = \frac{\pi \times 100^4}{64}\]
\[= \frac{\pi \times 100{,}000{,}000}{64} = \frac{3.1416 \times 10^8}{64}\]
\[I_{\text{solid}} = 4.91 \times 10^6 \text{ mm}^4\]

(b) Hollow circle — do = 100 mm, di = 60 mm

CALCULATION
\[I = \frac{\pi(d_o^4 - d_i^4)}{64} = \frac{\pi(100^4 - 60^4)}{64}\]
\[= \frac{\pi(10^8 - 1.296 \times 10^7)}{64} = \frac{\pi \times 8.704 \times 10^7}{64}\]
\[I_{\text{hollow}} = 4.27 \times 10^6 \text{ mm}^4\]
COMPARISON
SECTION AREA (mm²) I (mm⁴)
Solid circle 7,854 \(4.91 \times 10^6\)
Hollow circle 5,027 \(4.27 \times 10^6\)
Hollow / Solid 64% 87%

The hollow section keeps 87% of the bending resistance using only 64% of the material. The material removed from the middle was contributing very little to \(I\) — it sits close to the axis, where \(y^2\) is small. This is why tubes, hollow shafts, and pipes dominate engineering design: material near the centre is structurally cheap to remove.

⚠️ Pay attention

The annulus result \(I = \pi(d_o^4 - d_i^4)/64\) is not a new rule to memorise on its own — it is \(I_{\text{outer}} - I_{\text{inner}}\): the second moment of a solid circle at the outer diameter, minus the second moment of a solid circle at the inner diameter (the material you removed to create the hole). Both are calculated about the same centroidal axis before subtracting.

This is exactly the same subtraction logic you used in Section 1.1 to find the centroid of a shape with a hole, treating the hole as negative area. The pattern generalises: for any composite cross-section with a hole, calculate \(I\) as if the hole were not there, then subtract \(I\) of the removed region, about the same axis.

Where this stops applying: subtraction works here because \(I\) — like the first moment of area — is a moment integral, and integrals are linear: the integral of a sum (or difference) of regions equals the sum (or difference) of the integrals. This property extends to the polar moment \(J\) as well, since \(J\) is also \(\int r^2\,dA\). It does not extend to quantities that aren't moment integrals — you cannot subtract radii, stresses, or section moduli the same way. If a quantity isn't defined as \(\int (\text{something})\,dA\), do not assume this shortcut applies to it.

💡 Optional — want to know more? The product of second moment of area, \(I_{xy}\) ▾

Alongside \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) there is a third second-moment quantity, defined with the same units but a different integrand — the product of the two coordinates rather than the square of one:

Product of Second Moment of Area — Definition
\[I_{xy} = \int xy\, dA\]
\(I_{xy}\)Product of second moment of area about the centroidal \(x\)–\(y\) axes (units \(\text{mm}^4\))
\(x,\ y\)Coordinates of the area element \(dA\), measured from the centroid

Unlike \(I_x = \int y^2 dA\) and \(I_y = \int x^2 dA\), whose integrands are squares and therefore always positive, the integrand \(xy\) changes sign between quadrants. So \(I_{xy}\) can be positive, negative, or zero — it measures not how spread out the area is, but how diagonally skewed it is relative to the \(x\)–\(y\) axes.

The one rule worth remembering

If a cross-section has even one axis of symmetry, then for every element \(dA\) at \((x, y)\) there is a mirror element whose \(xy\) product has the opposite sign. The contributions cancel pair by pair, and:

Symmetry Rule
\[\text{Any axis of symmetry through the centroid} \;\Longrightarrow\; I_{xy} = 0\]

Every section used in this course — rectangles, circles, I-beams, T-sections, hollow tubes — has at least one axis of symmetry. That is why \(I_{xy}\) never appears in our calculations: for these shapes it is exactly zero, and \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) alone tell the full story. This is a deliberate choice in how the course is scoped, not an accident.

When and how \(I_{xy}\) must be applied

For a section with no axis of symmetry — an angle (L) section or a Z-section — \(I_{xy} \neq 0\), and this changes how the familiar formulas may be used:

  • The bending formula \(\sigma = My/I\) assumes \(I_{xy} = 0\). It is only valid for bending about a principal axis — an axis orientation for which \(I_{xy}\) vanishes. Applying it to a vertically loaded L-section gives a wrong answer (the section actually deflects sideways as well as down), not an approximate one.
  • Principal axes always exist. For any shape, the axes can be rotated to a special orientation where \(I_{xy} = 0\); the second moments about these axes, \(I_{max}\) and \(I_{min}\), are the largest and smallest the section can offer. Finding them uses \((I_x, I_y, I_{xy})\) together — via a rotation transformation or its graphical form, Mohr's circle for second moments of area. For symmetric sections, the symmetry axes are the principal axes, which is why we never needed this step.
  • Buckling uses the minimum principal \(I\). A strut buckles about its weakest axis. For an angle section, that is the diagonal principal axis, and \(I_{min}\) there is smaller than both \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) — using \(\min(I_x, I_y)\) instead would overestimate the buckling load, an error on the unsafe side.
  • It has its own Parallel Axis Theorem. Shifting to axes offset by \(d_x\) and \(d_y\) from the centroid: \(I_{xy} = \bar{I}_{xy} + A\, d_x d_y\) — note the offsets keep their signs, so this term can be negative.

You will not be assessed on \(I_{xy}\) in this course. What is worth carrying forward is the boundary it marks: the formulas you are learning apply to sections with an axis of symmetry. If you meet an unsymmetric section in later units or in practice — machine brackets, aircraft stringers, purlins — that is your cue that principal axes must be found first.
