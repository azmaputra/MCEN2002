Chapter 1 Properties of Plane Area

Polar Moment of Area

How area resists twisting rather than bending — and why it costs you nothing extra to compute once you already have \(I_x\) and \(I_y\).

Section 1.1 First Moment & Centroid Section 1.2 Second Moment of Area Section 1.3 Parallel Axis Theorem Section 1.4 Polar Moment Section 1.5 Summary
⬅️ Where you left off

In Section 1.3 you learned how \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) describe a section's resistance to bending about a horizontal or vertical axis — and how the Parallel Axis Theorem lets you build up the total for any composite shape. But a shaft under torsion isn't bending about an axis in the plane of the cross-section; it's twisting about the axis running through the section, perpendicular to it. That calls for a different distance — not \(y\) or \(x\) alone, but the full radial distance \(r\) from the centroid. The quantity that captures this is the polar moment of area, \(J\).

Core Concept

Resistance to twisting: the polar moment \(J\)

🎯 Why this concept

When a shaft is twisted, shear stress at any point depends on how far that point sits from the shaft's centre — not from a horizontal or vertical axis, but radially in every direction at once. \(J\) quantifies how area is distributed around the centroid as a point, rather than around a line. You'll use \(J\) directly in Chapter 4 to compute shear stress and angle of twist in rotating shafts, and again wherever torsional stiffness matters — drive shafts, fasteners, and torsion springs.

For any area element \(dA\) at coordinates \((x, y)\), its radial distance from the centroid is \(r\), where \(r^2 = x^2 + y^2\) by Pythagoras. The polar moment of area is defined the same way \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) were in Section 1.2 — a squared-distance integral over the area — except the distance used is \(r\) rather than \(x\) or \(y\):

Polar Moment of Area — Definition
\[J = \int r^2\, dA\]
\(J\)Polar moment of area about the centroid (units \(\text{mm}^4\) or \(\text{m}^4\))
\(r\)Radial distance from the centroid to the area element \(dA\)

Because \(r^2 = x^2 + y^2\), this integral splits cleanly into two pieces you already know how to compute — no new integration required. This relationship is called the Perpendicular Axis Theorem:

Perpendicular Axis Theorem
\[J = I_x + I_y\]
\(I_x\)Second moment of area about the centroidal \(x\)-axis — from Section 1.2
\(I_y\)Second moment of area about the centroidal \(y\)-axis — from Section 1.2
⚠️ The theorem only holds about the same centroidal point. \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) must both be taken about axes that pass through the section's own centroid and lie in the plane of the section. If you've already shifted \(I_x\) or \(I_y\) to some other axis using the Parallel Axis Theorem, you cannot add them to get \(J\) about that same offset point unless you apply the parallel axis shift to \(J\) itself, using the same offset distance in both directions.

Where does \(J = I_x + I_y\) come from?

Like the Parallel Axis Theorem, this isn't an approximation — it drops straight out of the definition of \(J\) once you substitute \(r^2 = x^2 + y^2\). The derivation is short.

Standard Results

\(J\) for solid and hollow circular sections

Circular sections are by far the most common shape you'll compute \(J\) for, since shafts are almost always round or tubular. Both results follow directly from \(J = I_x + I_y\) applied to the standard circle formulas from Section 1.2, where \(I_x = I_y = \pi d^4/64\) for a solid circle by symmetry.

Solid Circular Section
\[J = \frac{\pi d^4}{32} = \frac{\pi r^4}{2}\]
\(d\)Diameter of the solid shaft
\(r\)Radius of the solid shaft, \(r = d/2\)
Hollow Circular Section (Annulus)
\[J = \frac{\pi \left(d_o^4 - d_i^4\right)}{32}\]
\(d_o\)Outer diameter
\(d_i\)Inner diameter

Notice the hollow-section formula is just the solid formula for the outer diameter, minus the solid formula for the inner diameter — the same subtraction logic you used for holes when finding centroids in Section 1.1 and composite \(I_x\) in Section 1.3.

💡 Why are shafts round, not square? For a given cross-sectional area, a hollow circular shaft maximises \(J\) because every bit of material sits far from the centre, at a large and roughly uniform radial distance. A solid square shaft is comparatively inefficient — its corner material is close to the centroid along the diagonal but the section carries a lot of area near the middle that contributes little to \(J\). This is also why gaining strength "for free" by hollowing out a shaft works so well: material removed from near the centre barely reduces \(J\), while removing the same area from the outer edge would cost you dearly.
Related Property

The polar radius of gyration \(k\)

🎯 Why this concept

Values of \(J\) are large numbers in \(\text{mm}^4\) — useful for calculation, but hard to interpret and awkward to compare between sections of different sizes. The radius of gyration condenses \(J\) and the area \(A\) into a single length that tells you how far from the centroid the section's area is effectively concentrated. The same construction returns in Chapter 6, where \(k_x = \sqrt{I_x/A}\) governs column buckling through the slenderness ratio — so it's worth meeting the idea now, in its simplest polar form.

Imagine squeezing the entire area \(A\) into a thin ring at some radius \(k\) from the centroid, chosen so that the ring has exactly the same polar moment as the real section. Since all of the ring's area sits at the single distance \(k\), its polar moment is simply \(A k^2\). Setting this equal to the real section's \(J\) defines the polar radius of gyration:

Polar Radius of Gyration
\[k = \sqrt{\frac{J}{A}} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad J = A k^2\]
\(k\)Polar radius of gyration about the centroid (units \(\text{mm}\) or \(\text{m}\) — a length, not \(\text{mm}^4\))
\(J\)Polar moment of area about the centroid
\(A\)Cross-sectional area

A larger \(k\) means the section's material sits farther from the centre on average — exactly the quality that makes a section torsionally efficient. Two sections with equal area but different \(k\) will have very different \(J\): the one with the larger \(k\) wins, at no extra material cost.

⚠️ \(k\) is not a physical radius of the section. It is an effective (root-mean-square) distance — no actual boundary of the shape needs to lie at \(r = k\). For a solid circular shaft, \(k = r/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707\,r\), which is less than the outer radius because material fills the section all the way in to the centre. Watch the units too: \(k\) is in \(\text{mm}\), not \(\text{mm}^4\) — quoting \(J\) where \(k\) is asked for (or vice versa) is a common slip.

Quick check with two typical shafts

Applying \(k = \sqrt{J/A}\) to a solid \(60\text{ mm}\) shaft and an \(80/50\text{ mm}\) hollow shaft — the same two sections solved in full in the worked examples below:

\[\begin{aligned} \textbf{Solid, } d = 60\text{ mm:}\quad A &= \frac{\pi d^2}{4} = 2827\ \text{mm}^2, \qquad k = \sqrt{\frac{1.27\times10^{6}}{2827}} = 21.2\ \text{mm} \\[8pt] \textbf{Hollow, } d_o = 80,\ d_i = 50\text{ mm:}\quad A &= \frac{\pi\left(d_o^2 - d_i^2\right)}{4} = 3063\ \text{mm}^2, \qquad k = \sqrt{\frac{3.41\times10^{6}}{3063}} = 33.4\ \text{mm} \end{aligned}\]

The comparison is telling. The solid shaft's \(k\) is \(0.707\) of its \(30\text{ mm}\) outer radius, but the hollow shaft's \(k\) is \(0.83\) of its \(40\text{ mm}\) outer radius — its material sits proportionally farther out, which is the structural-efficiency argument from the callout above expressed as a single number.

Worked Example

Polar moment of a solid shaft

A solid circular shaft has diameter \(d = 60\text{ mm}\). Find its polar moment of area \(J\).

\[\begin{aligned} J &= \frac{\pi d^4}{32} \\ &= \frac{\pi \times 60^4}{32} \\ &= \frac{\pi \times 12{,}960{,}000}{32} \\ &= 1.27\times10^{6}\ \text{mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]

You could equally have found this via \(I_x = I_y = \pi d^4/64 = 0.636\times10^{6}\text{ mm}^4\) and summed them — \(J = I_x + I_y = 1.27\times10^{6}\text{ mm}^4\), the same result, confirming the Perpendicular Axis Theorem holds for the standard circle formula too.

Worked Example · Hollow Section

Hollow shaft: \(J\) by subtraction

Treat the hollow shaft as a solid outer circle with a solid inner circle removed — the same subtraction approach you used for holes in Section 1.1 and Section 1.3.

Page 1 · The problem

A hollow circular shaft has an outer diameter \(d_o = 80\text{ mm}\) and an inner diameter \(d_i = 50\text{ mm}\). Find the polar moment of area \(J\) for the cross-section.

  • Treat this as a solid \(80\text{ mm}\) shaft, with a solid \(50\text{ mm}\) shaft's worth of area removed from the centre
  • Both circles share the same centroid, so no parallel axis shift is needed — this is a pure subtraction
Page 2 · Compute each solid circle's \(J\)

Find \(J\) for the full outer circle and for the circle representing the removed material, using the solid-circle formula for each:

\[\begin{aligned} J_{outer} &= \frac{\pi d_o^4}{32} = \frac{\pi \times 80^4}{32} = 4.02\times10^{6}\ \text{mm}^4 \\[6pt] J_{inner} &= \frac{\pi d_i^4}{32} = \frac{\pi \times 50^4}{32} = 0.61\times10^{6}\ \text{mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]
Page 3 · Subtract

The hollow section's \(J\) is the outer solid circle's \(J\) minus the removed inner circle's \(J\):

\[\begin{aligned} J &= J_{outer} - J_{inner} \\ &= 4.02\times10^{6} - 0.61\times10^{6} \\ &= 3.41\times10^{6}\ \text{mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]

The hollow shaft retains about \(85\%\) of the solid \(80\text{ mm}\) shaft's \(J\), while using only about \(61\%\) of its material — this trade-off is exactly what the interactive comparison below lets you explore.
