Chapter Summary

What you now know — and why it matters

You've established the geometric foundation on which the rest of this course's structural analysis is built. Every one of these came from the same idea — an integral over the cross-sectional area, weighted by some power of distance — just applied to a different distance and a different power:

Property Symbol Definition Physical meaning Used in
Centroid \(\bar x, \bar y\) \(\bar y = \dfrac{\int y\, dA}{A}\) Geometric balance point; the reference every other calculation in this chapter is measured from Foundational — every later property depends on it
Second Moment of Area \(I_x, I_y\) \(I_x = \int y^2\, dA\) Resistance to bending; how far area is spread from the neutral axis Ch. 3 (bending stress), Ch. 6 (buckling)
Polar Moment of Area \(J\) \(J = \int r^2\, dA\)
\(= I_x + I_y\)		 Resistance to torsion; how far area is spread from the centroid point Ch. 4 (torsion, shear stress and angle of twist)

Notice the pattern: distance to the first power gives you the centroid (Section 1.1); distance squared, measured vertically or horizontally, gives \(I_x\) or \(I_y\) (Section 1.2); distance squared, measured radially, gives \(J\) (Section 1.4). Same idea, different distance.

Key Relationships

The four ideas worth carrying forward

  • Parallel Axis Theorem (Section 1.3): \(I_x = \bar I_x + Ad^2\) — always apply about the composite centroid, and only once you know where that centroid actually is
  • Perpendicular Axis Theorem (Section 1.4): \(J = I_x + I_y\) — only valid when \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) are taken about the same centroidal point
  • Material far from the reference contributes disproportionately — because every one of these quantities involves a squared distance, doubling an offset quadruples its contribution. This is the mathematical reason I-beam flanges and hollow shaft walls are structurally efficient
  • Holes are negative area (Section 1.1) — a hole subtracts from \(A\), \(Q\), \(I_x\), \(I_y\), and \(J\) alike; treat it as a "negative" sub-component in every composite calculation, using the same procedure as any other piece
Worked Example · Everything Together

One shape, all three properties

An L-shaped angle bracket is built from two rectangles: a vertical leg \(20\text{ mm} \times 100\text{ mm}\), and a horizontal leg \(60\text{ mm} \times 20\text{ mm}\) attached to its base, so the overall bracket spans \(80\text{ mm}\) wide by \(100\text{ mm}\) tall. Find the centroid, \(I_x\), \(I_y\), and \(J\) about the composite centroid — using every tool from this chapter in one pass.

Page 1 · The problem

Split the bracket into two non-overlapping rectangles, measuring all positions from the bottom-left corner of the bracket:

  • Rectangle A (vertical leg): width \(20\text{ mm}\), height \(100\text{ mm}\), spanning \(x: 0\text{–}20\), \(y: 0\text{–}100\)
  • Rectangle B (horizontal leg): width \(60\text{ mm}\), height \(20\text{ mm}\), spanning \(x: 20\text{–}80\), \(y: 0\text{–}20\)
\[A_1 = 20 \times 100 = 2000\text{ mm}^2 \qquad A_2 = 60 \times 20 = 1200\text{ mm}^2\]

Each rectangle's own centroid, measured from the bottom-left corner of the bracket:

\[(x_1, y_1) = (10, 50) \qquad (x_2, y_2) = (50, 10)\]
Page 2 · Composite centroid (Section 1.1)

Apply the weighted-average centroid formula in both directions:

\[\begin{aligned} \bar x &= \frac{A_1 x_1 + A_2 x_2}{A_1+A_2} = \frac{2000(10)+1200(50)}{3200} = 25\text{ mm} \\[6pt] \bar y &= \frac{A_1 y_1 + A_2 y_2}{A_1+A_2} = \frac{2000(50)+1200(10)}{3200} = 35\text{ mm} \end{aligned}\]

The composite centroid sits at \((\bar x, \bar y) = (25, 35)\text{ mm}\) — everything from here on is measured relative to this point.

Page 3 · \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) via the Parallel Axis Theorem (Section 1.3)

For each rectangle, find its own \(\bar I_x\) and \(\bar I_y\), the offset \(d\) to the composite centroid in each direction, then apply \(I = \bar I + Ad^2\):

\[\begin{aligned} \bar I_{x,1} &= \frac{20(100)^3}{12} = 1{,}666{,}667\text{ mm}^4 & d_{y,1} &= |50-35| = 15\text{ mm} \\ I_{x,1} &= 1{,}666{,}667 + 2000(15)^2 = 2{,}116{,}667\text{ mm}^4 \\[10pt] \bar I_{x,2} &= \frac{60(20)^3}{12} = 40{,}000\text{ mm}^4 & d_{y,2} &= |10-35| = 25\text{ mm} \\ I_{x,2} &= 40{,}000 + 1200(25)^2 = 790{,}000\text{ mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]
\[I_x = I_{x,1}+I_{x,2} = 2{,}116{,}667+790{,}000 = 2{,}906{,}667\text{ mm}^4\]

The same procedure, rotated 90°, gives \(I_y\):

\[\begin{aligned} \bar I_{y,1} &= \frac{100(20)^3}{12} = 66{,}667\text{ mm}^4 & d_{x,1} &= |10-25| = 15\text{ mm} \\ I_{y,1} &= 66{,}667 + 2000(15)^2 = 516{,}667\text{ mm}^4 \\[10pt] \bar I_{y,2} &= \frac{20(60)^3}{12} = 360{,}000\text{ mm}^4 & d_{x,2} &= |50-25| = 25\text{ mm} \\ I_{y,2} &= 360{,}000 + 1200(25)^2 = 1{,}110{,}000\text{ mm}^4 \end{aligned}\]
\[I_y = I_{y,1}+I_{y,2} = 516{,}667+1{,}110{,}000 = 1{,}626{,}667\text{ mm}^4\]
Page 4 · \(J\) via the Perpendicular Axis Theorem (Section 1.4)

With \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) both about the composite centroid, \(J\) is a single addition — no new integration, no new procedure:

\[J = I_x + I_y = 2{,}906{,}667 + 1{,}626{,}667 = 4{,}533{,}334\text{ mm}^4\]

Full result for this bracket, about its own composite centroid:

\[(\bar x, \bar y) = (25, 35)\text{ mm} \qquad I_x = 2.91\times10^{6}\text{ mm}^4 \qquad I_y = 1.63\times10^{6}\text{ mm}^4 \qquad J = 4.53\times10^{6}\text{ mm}^4\]

Every one of these came from the same two sub-components and the same weighted-sum logic — the centroid locates the reference point, the Parallel Axis Theorem builds up \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) about it, and the Perpendicular Axis Theorem gets \(J\) for free once \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) are known. That's the whole toolkit from this chapter, used once, in sequence.

Common Mistakes — Recap

What tends to go wrong

⚠️ Applying \(Ad^2\) before finding the composite centroid. \(d\) is only meaningful once you know where the composite centroid actually is — find it first, every time (Section 1.1, Section 1.3).
⚠️ Stopping at the composite centroidal \(I\) when the question asks about a different axis. "About the base" or "about an edge" needs one more parallel-axis shift beyond the composite centroid result (Section 1.3).
⚠️ Adding \(I_x\) and \(I_y\) that were taken about different points. The Perpendicular Axis Theorem only holds when both are about the same centroidal point (Section 1.4).
